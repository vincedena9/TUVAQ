About
✕

Tuvaq — landfast ice — is essential for Mittimatalingmiut (people of Mittimatalik), who travel on it for about seven months a year to hunt, fish, and connect with the land. But this ice is changing: forming later, breaking sooner, and becoming harder to predict.

Solar-powered cameras installed on Igarjuaq (Mount Herodier) and in Mittimatalik provide real-time images of sea ice conditions overlooking key travel routes across Tasiujaq and Tursukattak from early spring to late summer — helping local residents make safer decisions when navigating on sea ice, choosing routes, and planning trips to the floe edge.

The first camera system was deployed in 2015 following community concerns about vessel traffic breaking through winter ice. Hunters and travellers quickly found the images useful. We relaunched the project in 2025 with improved equipment and closer community involvement.

Beyond community use, the camera data helps scientists study how landfast ice forms, moves and breaks up, improving the sea ice models used in Arctic forecasting.

The project runs from 2025 to 2030, in collaboration with Oceans North Canada and with the support of the Mittimatalik Hunters and Trappers Organization (MHTO). Findings are shared openly, and we welcome anyone from Mittimatalik — especially youth and students — who wants to get involved.
